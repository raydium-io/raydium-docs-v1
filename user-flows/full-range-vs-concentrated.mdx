## What "range" means in CLMM

Every CLMM position has a lower price bound and an upper price bound. Your liquidity is only active — only earns fees — while the current market price is between those two bounds.

The narrower your range, the more concentrated your capital and the higher your fee share per dollar deposited — but the more likely you are to go out of range. The wider your range, the lower your concentration and fee APR, but the longer you can leave the position alone.

## Full-range positions

A full-range CLMM position sets the lower bound to the minimum possible price and the upper bound to the maximum. It behaves almost identically to a CPMM deposit:

- Capital is spread across all prices.
- It never goes out of range.
- Capital efficiency is low — similar to CPMM.
- No monitoring or rebalancing required.

CPMM is usually simpler and preferred for plain passive LP. Full-range CLMM is not meaningfully better than CPMM for an ordinary deposit.

## Concentrated positions

A tighter range puts more of your capital to work near the current price. This increases the fees you earn per dollar while price stays in range — but it also means you go out of range faster and need to rebalance more often.

The key trade-off: concentration multiplies both fee income and management burden.

## Choosing a range in practice

Match the width of your range to how much the pair actually moves and how often you're willing to rebalance:

- **Stable pairs (USDC/USDT):** the tightest ranges work well. These pairs barely move, so a narrow band gives high capital efficiency with very little out-of-range risk.
- **Correlated pairs (jitoSOL/SOL, mSOL/SOL):** a moderate range. They drift slowly as staking yield accrues but rarely diverge sharply; rebalance when a bound is approached.
- **Volatile majors (SOL/USDC):** wider ranges. The wider you go, the less you rebalance but the lower your fee concentration — settle on the point of that trade-off you can actually keep up with.
- **Long-tail / memecoin pairs:** a very wide range, or just use CPMM. Tight ranges on highly volatile pairs spend most of their life out of range.

The narrower the range, the more you earn while in range and the more often you fall out of it. Choose the width you can monitor, not the one that maximises theoretical APR.

## Out-of-range behaviour

When price exits your range:

- **Below lower bound:** your position is now 100% token A. No fees earned.
- **Above upper bound:** your position is now 100% token B. No fees earned.

The position is not lost — you can withdraw or wait for price to return. IL stops changing once you are fully out of range.

## Rebalancing

When price approaches or exits a range boundary:

1. **Wait.** If you expect price to return, doing nothing preserves your position.
2. **Rebalance.** Close the current position, swap tokens to the new ratio, and open a new position centred on the current price.

There is no in-place range adjustment. Rebalancing always means closing and reopening.
