<Info>
  Providing liquidity is not free money. You earn fees, but in exchange you take on price exposure that can leave you worse off than simply holding the two tokens. This page is about deciding whether that trade is worth it for a given pair and position size.
</Info>

## What you earn

You deposit a pair of tokens, get a position back, and that position earns from up to two sources:

**Swap fees — the base return.** Every trade through your pool pays a fee (typically 0.01%–1%, set by the pool's tier), shared among LPs by how much of the pool each one owns. It builds up for as long as the pool has volume. In a **CPMM** pool the fees compound into the reserves, so your position is simply worth more when you withdraw; in a **CLMM** pool they sit as a separate balance you claim when you want.

**Farm rewards — an optional top-up.** Some pools add token incentives funded by a project's treasury or by Raydium. You stake your LP tokens to earn them on top of swap fees. They're finite — fixed schedule, and the APR falls as more LPs join — so treat them as a bonus, not the reason to enter.

## The trade-off: fees vs. impermanent loss

As people trade against your pool, it rebalances itself, selling whichever token is going up and buying whichever is going down. You end up with more of the token that fell and less of the one that rose, so your position is worth less than if you'd just kept the two tokens in your wallet. That difference is **impermanent loss (IL)**. It's called "impermanent" because it goes away if prices come back to where you started, but you shouldn't count on that, so it's best treated as a real cost.

IL applies to every pool type, and it's what ultimately decides whether LPing pays off. There's a full breakdown with worked numbers on [LP risks](/user-flows/lp-risks).

So it all comes down to one comparison:

> **Do the swap fees you collect outweigh the impermanent loss you incur?**

Whether they do depends on three things: how much the two tokens' prices move apart (more divergence → more IL), how much volume the pool does relative to its TVL (more volume → more fees), and the pool's fee tier.

## When LPing beats holding

LPing tends to come out ahead when:

- **The pair is correlated.** For tokens that move together — stable/stable, LST/SOL, wrapped variants — prices barely diverge, IL stays small, and fees turn the position clearly positive.
- **The pool has genuine volume.** Fees only exist if people trade. A \$10M pool doing \$1M/day at 0.25% earns roughly 9% APR across its LPs; an identical pool with no traders earns nothing. Always check 24-hour volume against TVL before depositing.
- **Fee income clears the IL over your holding period.** A high enough fee tier on a pair that actually moves can more than cover the divergence.

LPing tends to lose to holding when:

- **You LP a volatile/volatile pair with thin volume** (e.g. a memecoin against SOL). Price divergence outruns the trickle of fees, and IL dominates.
- **You run a tight CLMM range but don't rebalance.** The position drifts out of range, earns nothing, and still carries the IL banked at the boundary.
- **You chase a headline farm APR without checking when it ends.** A 300% APR farm with two weeks left on the schedule is not a 300% return.

## How much can you expect?

There is no single "LP return" — it depends entirely on the pair you pick and how you manage the position. A few representative approaches:

| Approach | Example pool | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Stable pair** | USDC/USDT, CLMM 0.01% tight range | Steady, modest fees — the closest thing to "set and earn." |
| **Correlated pair** | jitoSOL/SOL, CLMM 0.05% medium range | Solid fees with light monitoring. |
| **Major volatile, passive** | SOL/USDC, CPMM 0.25% | Earns at any price, no management. |
| **Major volatile, active** | SOL/USDC, CLMM 0.25% range | Top fee capture while in range, but frequent rebalancing and amplified IL. |
| **Long-tail / new token** | New token, CPMM 1% | High headline fees, but closer to a directional bet on the token. |

Returns scale with trading volume, your share of the pool, time-in-range (for CLMM), and how far prices move. Treat any APR figure you see as a snapshot, not a promise.

The bottom line versus holding: holding keeps your full upside but earns nothing, while LPing trades away some of that upside (IL) in return for fees. On a correlated pair with steady volume, that's often the best risk-adjusted yield on-chain. On a speculative pair, the fees rarely cover the IL — and you'd have been better off just holding.
